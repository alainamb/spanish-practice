Commands Practice - Tú & Usted

Introduction

Giving commands in Spanish requires knowing when to use the familiar tú form versus the formal usted form, and how to construct both affirmative (do this) and negative (don't do this) commands. This activity gives you practice forming commands in realistic, everyday situations where you might need to give instructions, make requests or suggestions, or offer advice to others.

Commands are one of the most practical aspects of Spanish communication—you'll use them when giving directions, teaching someone a task, following instructions, or simply asking someone to do (or not do) something!

Instructions

How this activity works:

  1. First, select a practice mode: Familiar Commands (Tú) for friends, family, children, and peers, or Formal Commands (Usted) for strangers, authority figures, and people you want to show respect toward.
  2. Read the scenario carefully to understand the context.
  3. Click either Positive Command (telling someone to do something) or Negative Command (telling someone not to do something).
  4. The answer will appear showing you the correct command form for that situation.
  5. Click Next Scenario to practice with a new situation.

Verbs you'll practice: ir, ver, decir, hacer, poner, salir, ser, tener, venir, hablar, ofrecer, traducir, traer, llevar, dar, comer, escuchar, abrir, cerrar, sentarse, quedarse, probar, añadir, interrumpir, tirar, bajar, guardar, olvidarse, preocuparse, dudar, and participar.

Quick tips for forming commands:

  • Tú affirmative commands: Use the él/ella form of the present tense (with 8 major irregular exceptions: di, haz, ve, pon, sal, sé, ten, ven)
  • Tú negative commands: Take the yo form of the present tense, drop the -o, and add -es (for -ar verbs) or -as (for -er/-ir verbs). Example: hablo → no hables
  • Usted commands (both positive and negative): Take the yo form, drop the -o, and add -e (for -ar verbs) or -a (for -er/-ir verbs). For negative, just add no in front. Example: hablo → hable / no hable
  • Reflexive verbs: Attach the pronoun to the end of affirmative commands (siéntate, quédese) but place it before negative commands (no te sientes, no se quede)

Remember: Context matters! The same scenario might call for different levels of formality depending on your relationship with the person you're addressing.
